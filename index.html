Track: Sr. Director of Software Engineering — Platform Engineering · AI-Enabled SDLC · Regulated Delivery · DevSecOps · Engineering Operating Model
Q1 Walk me through your most significant engineering leadership achievement. BehavioralLeadership ▼
Open with: "I'll use Foundation Medicine as my proof of concept — because it combines every dimension this question is really asking about: scale, complexity, regulated environment, and measurable business impact."
  • Grew Quality & Compliance Engineering from 15 → 55 FTEs under $6M budget over 10 years — built four distinct capabilities: QE, compliance engineering, SDLC governance, systems integration
  • Built and chaired the SDLC Council — executive governance board aligning engineering, quality, compliance, and business on critical delivery priorities
  • Delivered AI-powered SDLC intelligence: 100% requirement-to-deployment traceability, 92% release-risk prediction accuracy
  • Outcomes: 90% audit prep reduction, 40% release frequency increase, 99.99% uptime, zero regulatory findings
  • This was not just delivery — it was org design, governance architecture, AI operationalization, and cultural transformation simultaneously
Close with: "The measure of this achievement isn't the metrics — it's that the organization continued to improve after systems and people were in place. That's the sign you've built something that lasts."
Q2 How do you build and scale an engineering organization? LeadershipStrategic ▼
Open with: "Scaling an engineering org is a systems design problem, not a hiring problem. You design the structure first — then hire into it."
  • Start with mission, vision, operating model — not just headcount. Define what the org is for before adding people to it
  • Design the org topology to match delivery needs: functional vs product-aligned, platform teams vs stream-aligned teams
  • Build the governance layer early: SDLC Council, operating rhythm (MBR/QBR), escalation model — structure enables speed, not the reverse
  • Hire leaders before ICs at scale — the right Directors compound; wrong ones create technical debt in the org
  • Measure maturity, not just output: DORA metrics, compliance posture, team health, delivery predictability
  • 15 → 55 FTEs: grew four distinct capabilities as the business evolved — was never just headcount, always a capability expansion
Close with: "A scaled org isn't bigger — it's more capable. Every hire should make the system smarter, not just larger."
Q3 How do you approach platform engineering and developer experience? Technical ▼
Open with: "Platform engineering is the highest-leverage investment an engineering leader can make. You build once for the whole org — the return compounds with every team that uses it."
  • Built reusable integration patterns, CI/CD golden paths, and deployment standards across Foundation Medicine's regulated platform environment
  • Golden paths embed compliance controls by default — teams don't choose between speed and compliance, the path enforces both
  • Outcome: 40% increase in release frequency as a direct result of platform investment — not despite compliance, because of automated compliance
  • Toolchain: GitHub Actions, Jenkins, OpenTelemetry, Grafana — commodity infrastructure + custom governance layer on top for differentiation
  • Platform teams must measure developer experience: DORA metrics, deployment lead time, test cycle time, platform adoption rate
Close with: "The platform team's job is to make the right thing the easy thing. When that's true, compliance becomes invisible to the developer — it just works."
Q4 How do you use AI to improve engineering operations? TechnicalStrategic ▼
Open with: "I approach AI in engineering at three levels simultaneously: AI in the product, AI in the operations, and AI in the governance. Most leaders only think about the first one."
  • AI in the product: Launched AI-assisted application experience using Claude — helping users understand clinical reports, workflow status, and operational context
  • AI in operations: Built AI-powered release-risk prediction model at 92% accuracy — teams assess risk before a release, not after a failure
  • AI in governance: Operationalized AI-powered SDLC intelligence — 100% traceability, automated evidence capture, auditable AI outputs
  • Authored the enterprise AI policy for Foundation Medicine — Roche-aligned, FDA-relevant, practical not theoretical
  • Combined outcome: AI enabled reliability, compliance, and velocity simultaneously — not as separate initiatives, as a unified strategy
Close with: "AI applied to operations is a force multiplier — but only if you govern it. The 92% prediction accuracy is only useful if the organization trusts the output. Governance builds that trust."
Q5 Describe your approach to engineering governance without slowing teams down. StrategicLeadership ▼
Open with: "The false premise is that governance and speed are in tension. They're not — bad governance slows teams. Good governance removes friction and ambiguity."
  • Built the SDLC Council — not a committee, a decision-making engine. It aligned 5 functions around one set of delivery priorities and removed ambiguity from every team below it
  • Standardized governance through golden paths and policy-as-code — teams follow standards automatically, not manually. Automation removes the friction
  • Result: 40% more releases AND 90% less audit prep simultaneously — the same governance investment produced both outcomes
  • Key principle: governance as infrastructure, not oversight. When controls are automated, engineers don't fight them — they don't notice them
  • Governance must have executive teeth — a policy without enforcement is a suggestion. The SDLC Council had decision rights, not just advisory influence
Close with: "When stakeholders trust the data and trust the process, they stop micromanaging delivery. The SDLC Council was the mechanism for building that trust at scale."
Q6 How do you lead a team through a major technical transformation? BehavioralLeadership ▼
Open with: "Transformation fails when leaders treat it as a project. It succeeds when leaders design it as a capability shift — the journey changes the org, not just the technology."
  • FMI transformation: DevOps and cloud-native adoption across a regulated, FDA-aligned engineering organization — technically demanding and culturally challenging
  • Sequence: vision → governance → golden paths → adoption → measurement — each step created the conditions for the next
  • Change management was built into the platform: golden paths made the new way the easy way — no willpower required from teams
  • Embedded QE into DevOps transformation — QE became a delivery accelerator, not a quality gate. Changed the team's identity
  • Measured the transformation continuously: DORA baseline → target → achieved — teams could see their own progress
Close with: "The hardest part of transformation isn't technology — it's identity. When the team starts saying 'this is how we work' instead of 'this is what we're being asked to do', you've succeeded."
Q7 How do you manage executive stakeholders and build alignment? StrategicLeadership ▼
Open with: "Executive alignment isn't a conversation — it's a system. You build the forums, the cadence, the shared language, and the data. Then alignment happens continuously, not episodically."
  • The SDLC Council was the system: monthly executive forum with engineering, quality, compliance, and business — shared data, shared priorities, shared accountability
  • Executives don't want updates — they want confidence. Confidence comes from consistent, predictable, data-driven reporting
  • Built executive dashboards showing compliance posture, reliability, delivery health, and risk — no surprises, always current
  • Managed upward to VP and C-suite on platform modernization, AI governance, and regulatory posture simultaneously
  • Key insight: executive alignment is a system, not a skill — build the forums, cadence, and shared language so it works without you having to broker every decision
Close with: "When stakeholders trust the data and trust the process, they stop micromanaging delivery. The SDLC Council was the mechanism for building that trust at scale."
Q8 How do you make build vs. buy decisions? TechnicalStrategic ▼
Open with: "My framework is: build where it differentiates your business, buy where it commoditizes. The hard work is being honest about which is which."
  • Evaluate: strategic differentiation, time to value, TCO, regulatory fit, vendor lock-in risk, integration burden
  • QMS platform: evaluated build vs Veeva. Chose Veeva + compliance config layer — faster to value, proven regulatory track record, reduced build risk in FDA environment
  • CI/CD: chose standard tooling (GitHub Actions, Jenkins) for commodity pipeline + custom governance layer on top for compliance differentiation
  • SDLC intelligence: built internally because the use case (predicting release risk from our specific data) was proprietary and differentiated — no vendor could offer it
  • Key test: "Would a competitor buying this same tool close the gap on us?" If yes, buy. If no, build.
Close with: "The mistake most engineering leaders make is building everything because it feels more in control. I've built where it mattered and bought where it didn't — and the outcomes prove it."
Q9 Tell me about a time a project or system failed. What did you do? Behavioral ▼
Open with: "I'll answer this honestly — and I'll also tell you what systemic change it produced."
  • Use a real incident framing: a production reliability event, a missed release deadline, or a compliance gap discovered during audit
  • Structure: Situation → What failed → What I did → What changed systemically
  • Built reliability-by-design practices (OpenTelemetry, Grafana, risk-based controls) in direct response to incident patterns
  • Built AI-powered release-risk prediction (92% accuracy) — explicitly because reactive incident response was not enough; needed to predict and prevent
  • Established postmortem culture with blameless RCA → corrective action → systemic change loop
  • Sustained 99.99% uptime as proof that those systemic changes worked
Close with: "The best engineering leaders don't prevent all failures — they build systems that make failures smaller, shorter, and less likely to repeat."
Say "we never had failures" or deflect. Interviewers want intellectual honesty and evidence of learning systems.
Q10 What's your engineering leadership philosophy? Leadership ▼
Open with: "I lead with systems thinking — my job is to design the environment where great engineers do great work, not to be the smartest person in the room."
  • Systems over heroics: Build platforms, standards, and operating models that scale — don't rely on individuals to compensate for broken systems
  • Governance as enablement: The SDLC Council wasn't bureaucracy — it was the mechanism that accelerated decisions and removed ambiguity
  • Measure everything: 40% velocity improvement, 90% audit reduction, 99.99% uptime, 92% prediction accuracy — if you can't measure it, you can't improve it
  • Compliance as architecture: Security and compliance embedded into design, not layered on top — this is what enables speed in regulated environments
  • Grow the bench: 15 → 55 FTEs wasn't just headcount — it was building leaders who could lead leaders
  • AI-native thinking: Applied AI to operations, product, and governance simultaneously — not as a project, as a capability
Close with: "Ultimately, my job is to make the org more capable than I found it — and leave behind systems and people that don't need me to function."

Architecture patterns define how systems are structured and how components interact. Every engineering leader needs fluency in microservices, event-driven, API-first, and cloud-native patterns — and when to use monoliths vs distributed systems.
Pattern / StyleDefinitionKey BenefitsWatch-Outs

Monolith		Single deployable unit — all modules in one codebaseSimpler dev/test, no network calls, easier initial deliveryScaling bottlenecks, deployment risk, coupling between modules
Event-Driven Architecture		Components communicate via events (asynchronous messages), not direct callsDecoupling, resilience, real-time processing, audit trailEventual consistency, debugging complexity, dead-letter handling

Architecture Decision Framework: Monolith vs Microservices
Start with a Monolith when...

Early-stage or unclear domain

Team is small, domain boundaries not established, speed to market is critical, and operational complexity is a burden you can't yet afford.

Move to Microservices when...

Scale and team autonomy demand it

You have clear bounded contexts, teams own distinct domains, you're hitting scaling bottlenecks, and you have the platform maturity (K8s, observability, CI/CD) to support distributed systems.

Strangler Fig Pattern

Migrate gradually

Wrap the legacy monolith with new microservices at the boundary. Redirect traffic incrementally. The monolith "strangled" over time. Reduces risk of big-bang rewrites.
